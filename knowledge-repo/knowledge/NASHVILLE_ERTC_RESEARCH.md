# Nashville Eviction Right to Counsel (ERTC) — Research Brief for TenantGuard

## Executive Summary

Nashville's Metro Council is voting **tonight (June 16, 2026)** on a budget that includes **$2.1 million in additional funding** for the Eviction Right to Counsel (ERTC) program. This brings total ERTC funding to **$4.5 million** for FY2027, transitioning from federal ARPA money to the Metro general fund. The program needs **$9.3 million annually** for full implementation. TenantGuard's document intake, AI analysis, and attorney-matching platform could extend ERTC's capacity without proportional cost increases.

---

## The $2.1 Million Allocation

**What happened:** Metro Council Budget & Finance Committee Chair **Kyonzté Toombs** (District 2) filed a substitute budget proposal on June 12, 2026, reallocating $2.1 million to the ERTC program as part of the FY2027 budget. The full Metro Council votes on this tonight (June 16).

**Context:**
- Mayor Freddie O'Connell's original $3.8 billion FY2027 budget already included some ERTC funding
- The $2.1M is an *additional* allocation on top of the mayor's proposal
- Total ERTC funding will be **$4.5 million** (up from ~$1.8M in 2025)
- The program is now transitioning from ARPA (American Rescue Plan Act) federal funds to **Metro general fund operating budget** — making it permanent
- Full implementation would cost **$9.3 million annually** (per Stout evaluation)

**Key quote from Mayor O'Connell's office:**
> "Eviction Right to Counsel will now be funded by the general fund operating budget instead of federal emergency money."

**Ordinance:** BL2026-1382 — formally codifies the ERTC program under the **Housing Division of Metro Planning**

## Pilot Program Results (2025 — Stout Independent Evaluation)

The financial advisory firm **Stout** conducted an independent evaluation covering January–December 2025:

| Metric | Result |
|--------|--------|
| Households assisted | 1,069 |
| Program cost | ~$1.8 million |
| Total public fiscal benefits | $4.3 million |
| Return per $1 invested (fiscal) | $2.32 |
| Return per $1 invested (total) | $4.18 |
| Clients avoiding displacement | 93% |
| Case goals achieved | 87% |
| "Prevent eviction" goal achieved | 92% |
| Children in assisted households | 1,032 |
| Households kept stably housed | 370 |
| Average direct financial benefit per client | $12,000 |
| Median direct financial benefit | $7,800 |

**Fiscal savings breakdown:**

| Category | Amount |
|----------|--------|
| Housing social safety net (shelters, rapid rehousing) | $3.2M |
| Retained economic value (minimizing out-migration) | $310K |
| Avoided criminal justice costs | $210K |
| Reduced Medicaid spending | $140K |
| Improved educational attainment | $130K |
| Employment stability | $100K |
| Retained MNPS school funding | $70K |
| Avoided criminalizing homelessness | $60K |
| **Total** | **~$4.3M** |

**Demographics of clients served:**
- 77% female
- 65% Black/African American
- 40% at or below federal poverty level
- 56% had at least one child
- 33% reported having a disability
- 30% said they would face unsheltered homelessness if evicted
- 69% owed two months or less in back rent
- 38% cited job loss/reduced hours as reason for falling behind

## Current Crisis Level

- Nashville saw **1,000+ eviction filings per month** in March and April 2026
- This is **145% higher** than pre-pandemic eviction filings
- ERTC served 1,069 households in ALL of 2025 — roughly one month's worth of current filings
- The program has had to **turn away people** due to capacity constraints
- Tennessee's Emergency Rental Assistance Program (ERA-EPP) **permanently closed July 31, 2025**
- DOGE pulled federal funding that supported Nashville's eviction prevention programs

## How TenantGuard Fits In

### The Gap TenantGuard Can Fill

The ERTC program's biggest constraint is **capacity** — they served 1,069 households in a year while Nashville sees 1,000+ evictions per month. Even with $4.5M in funding, they cannot serve everyone.

**TenantGuard's value proposition to the ERTC coalition:**

1. **Pre-qualification triage** — AI-powered document analysis can instantly identify notice type, deadlines, and urgency level, allowing Legal Aid to prioritize cases
2. **Document preparation** — Tenants arrive at Legal Aid with organized, complete case files instead of scattered papers, reducing the 3-5 hour attorney setup time
3. **Capacity multiplier** — If TenantGuard handles initial intake and document organization, each ERTC attorney can serve more clients
4. **24/7 availability** — Tenants get immediate guidance at 2am when they find the notice, not just during business hours
5. **Self-service for simpler cases** — Some tenants may only need to understand their notice and know their deadline; TenantGuard can handle these without consuming attorney time
6. **Data and reporting** — Platform analytics can help ERTC demonstrate impact to Metro Council for future funding

### Pitch Angle

> "For every dollar you invest in TenantGuard integration, you can extend your capacity to serve 3-5x more households. We handle the intake, document analysis, and case preparation — your attorneys focus on representation."

### Who to Approach First

1. **Elizabeth Leiserson** (ERTC Project Director at Legal Aid) — She's the operational leader who knows the capacity constraints firsthand
2. **DarKenya W. Waller** (Legal Aid Executive Director) — For partnership-level discussions
3. **Lisa Wooley** (Rooftop Nashville) — As a non-legal partner, she may be most receptive to tech solutions
